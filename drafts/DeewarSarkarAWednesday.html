One of the misconceptions about Hindi-language cinema is that it is by definition provincial. Hindi-language films are made for a Hindi-language audience about themes only relatable to Hindi/Urdu-speaking South Asians, who evidently do a lot of singing and dancing. It is locally-minded escapism for people who ‘lead lives of quiet desperation’ and die young and poor.

Dewaar (197X) flies in the face of such notions. Urban in setting, cosmopolitan in look and feel, violent, aggressive, and angry, Dewaar took the pulse of the nation and expressed frustrations, anxieties, and aspirations in a time of crisis and flux. This is the role which made AB a star and cemented his cinematic identity as the Angry Young Man of the Hindi-language screen.

Live fast, die young, bad girls do it well
… Dewaar is not a provincial film. It is not a film about living a life of quiet desperation, and it is not a film about dying quietly. Dewaar is about the freedom and temptations of urban living, reveling in the consumption of cosmopolitan modernity, and fighting for as much as you can get. The world is unjust, and the wicked are punished- but better than doing nothing is to struggle.
