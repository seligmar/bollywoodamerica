As summer turns to fall the season of horror, victoriana, and film noir arrives. My grandmother was a fan of noir- of film in general- and some of my fondest childhood memories are of curling up on the couch with her after dinner and watching films like Laura (1944) and The Big Sleep (1946), probably before they were age-appropriate. C.I.D.(1956) falls into this same family of moody urban dramas, full of dark shadows, dark secrets, and dark passions, and the whisper of something dangerous and intoxicating in the air.
C.I.D. begins with a close-up on a telephone set in shadow, quickly cutting to a man whose face we never see; a young woman; a man in a bar, who turns to an accomplice, passing along a message. Within moments our stage is set: a murky villian, a femme fatal, a criminal network, and a murder. The victim is the editor of "The Bomabay Times", who is stabbed to death moments before the arrival of C.I.D. Inspector Shekahr. Recalling seeing a man exiting the elevator on the way to the editor's office, Inspector Shekar gives chase, comindeering the car of a young woman but ultimately loosing the killer. And so within 15 minutes the stage is set: a murder, a potential love triange, a herioc cop and an underworld, Bombay after dark in the rain.
The is no agreed-upon definition for film noir, although moral ambiquity, sex, and a stylised atmosphere are generally considered requisite components. Although nowhere near as dark as other offerings in the genre, C.I.D. certainly counts. That said, C.I.D. lacks complexity. There are plot twists, but there is never any real concern that Inspector Shekar will die, or turn out to be the villian, or fail to get the girl in the end. The "right" girl, mind you, not the femme fatal- perish the thought. C.I.D. was, however, a hit when released, so evidently the viewing public didnt share my view. My opinion is that if it looks like a duck and it quacks like a duck, this is film studies and not organic chemistry. (Science is real #MarchForScience insert image)
Guru Dutt's production company brought C.I.D to the screen, although Dutt did not himself direct, which is unfortunate: Dutt has since become famous for his directorial genuis as much as for his tragic personal life. Although the director has a fine sense for the interplay between light and dark (chiaroscuro!) there is no doubt in my mind that this film could be better.
