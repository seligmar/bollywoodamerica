As summer turns to fall the season of horror, victoriana, and film noir arrives. My grandmother was a fan of noir- of film in general- and some of my fondest childhood memories are of curling up on the couch with her after dinner and watching films like Laura (1944) and The Big Sleep (1946), probably before they were age-appropriate. C.I.D. (1956) falls into this same family of moody urban dramas, full of dark shadows, dark secrets, and dark passions, and the whisper of something dangerous and intoxicating in the air.
C.I.D. begins with a close-up on a telephone set in shadow, quickly cutting to a man whose face we never see; a young woman; a man in a bar, who turns to an accomplice, passing along a message. Within moments our stage is set: a murky villian, a femme fatal, a criminal network, and a murder. The victim is the editor of "The Bomabay Times", who is stabbed to death moments before the arrival of C.I.D. Inspector Shekahr. Recalling seeing a man exiting the elevator on the way to the editor's office, Inspector Shekar gives chase, comindeering the car of a young woman but ultimately loosing the killer in the fantastically atmospheric Bombay after dark in the rain.

The is no agreed-upon definition for film noir, although moral ambiquity and a stylised atmosphere are generally considered requisite components. Although nowhere near as cynical as other offerings in the genre, C.I.D. certainly hits any and all qualifying stylistic beats. Director Raj Khosla has a fine sense for the interplay between light and dark (chiaroscuro!) and use of sound to set the tone. He also uses ambient street lights in Bombay to great effect, and shadows, and the preference for vertical and oblique lines to create those shadows and sense of clautrauphobia, is excellent.

German Expressionism, and the striking visuals of the movement, provided direct inspiration for noir, as well as a lot of the talent via film-industry professionals who got their start in Germany before the Second World War and emigrated to the US to work in Hollywood. For anyone who has yet to see it, The Cabinet of Dr. Chaligari (1920) is absolutely gorgeous.

That said, to my mind C.I.D. will never be among the best noirs because it simply isnt dark enough- surreal as it is to write as much in 2020. It lacks complexity and genuine suspense, and what Schrader describes as, "a fear of the future".1 There are plot twists, but there is never any real concern that Inspector Shekar will die, or turn out to be the villian, or fail to get the girl in the end, or even loose his job. Sure, people suck, but the system is ultimately working for the little guy for the greater good. This is in contrast to Notorios (1946), in which the U.S. Government pimps out Ingrid Bergman to an acutal Nazi.
Guru Dutt's production company brought C.I.D to the screen, although Dutt did not himself direct. Dutt has since become famous as much as for his tragic personal life as for his directorial genuis. While this film was made early in his career, presumably before his demons truly got their claws in deep, I suspect Dutt could have dug out some of the world-weary cynicism that makes Pyaasa, so immenintly watchable only a year earlier. That said, unlike Pyaasa, C.I.D. was a hit when released.
Maybe in this case, the medium truly is the message.
