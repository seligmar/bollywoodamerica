Our Femme Fatal

Like gothic horror, film noir has crept into western cultural consciousness to become immediately recognizable in ways that extend beyond celuloid into the tangible world around us. A fews months ago a new shampoo arrived in my Birchbox, and it filed my bathroom with a heady, old-fashioned musky smell that evoked a femme fatal as effectively as victorian mourning jewelry evokes a vampire's latest victim. My American grandmother was a fan of noir- of film in general- and some of my fondest childhood memories are of curling up on the couch with her after dinner and watching films like Laura  (1944) and The Big Sleep  (1946). C.I.D.  (1956) falls into this same family of moody urban dramas, full of dark shadows, dark secrets, and dark passions, and the whisper of something dangerous in the air.

C.I.D. begins with a close-up on a telephone set in shadow, quickly cutting to a man whose face we never see; a young woman; a man in a bar, who turns to an accomplice, passing along a message. Within moments our stage is set: a murky villian, a femme fatal, a criminal network, and a murder. The victim is the editor of "The Bomabay Times", who is stabbed to death moments before the arrival of C.I.D. Inspector Shekahr. Recalling seeing a man exiting the elevator on the way to the editor's office, Inspector Shekar gives chase, comindeering the car of a young woman but ultimately loosing the killer in the fantastically atmospheric Bombay after dark in the rain.

Bombay in the Rain

C.I.D. Inspector Shekahr is played by rising star Dev Anand, after he and friend Guru Dutt made a deal between themselves about teaming up on one anothers' films. As Edwina D'souza pointed out, this film launched several careers, in addition to brightening Anand's star. Costume designer Bhanu Athaiya's first film was C.I.D. Athaiya went on to design the costumes for over 100 films including Guide  (1965) Lagaan  , and Guru Dutt's now-revered classics. She won an Academy Award for her costume design for Ghandi  (1982)

Dutt also cast the erethrally beautiful Raheeda Rehman in her first Hindi-language film as his femme fatal before she went on to star in Pyaasa

That said, to my mind C.I.D. will never be among the best noirs because it simply isnt dark enough. It lacks complexity and genuine suspense, and what Schrader describes as, "a fear of the future".1 There are plot twists, but there is never any real concern that Inspector Shekar will die, or turn out to be the villian, or fail to get the girl in the end, or even loose his job. Sure, people suck, but the system is ultimately working for the little guy for the greater good. There is also a very jarring interlude with happily singing and dancing vilage women who may belong to a tribal community? For contrast, in the fantastic noir (or noir-ish) Notorios (1946) the U.S. Government pimps out Ingrid Bergman to a Nazi.

Dancing Vilager

Guru Dutt's production company brought C.I.D to the screen, although Dutt did not himself direct. Dutt has since become famous as much as for his tragic personal life as for his directorial genuis. While this film was made early in his career, perhaps before his demons truly got their claws in deep, I suspect Dutt could have dug out some world-weary cynicism- after all, he made Pyaasa only a year later. That said, C.I.D. was a hit when released, and the money earned from it and other hits enabled Dutt to pursue passion projects like Kaagaz Ke Phool ().

Maybe in this case, the medium truly is the message.
