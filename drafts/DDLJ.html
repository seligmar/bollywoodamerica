Dilwale Dulhania Le Jayenge(1995), commonly known by its initialism DDLJ, helped set the tone of Bollywood film in the 1990s and 2000s, and especially films targeted to Non-Resident Indians (NRIs), simply by being ubiquitous. Indeed, DDLJ is still being shown daily at the Maratha Mandir cinema hall in Mumbai.

--> And DDLJ is entertaining, there is no denying it. The train scene has been borrowed by several other films, including Jab We Met (2007), probably because it is adorable and iconic. Kajol's towel dance was followed up by Ranbir Kapoor's towel dance in Saawariya (), proof that every cloud has a well-chiseled, well-oiled, silver-in-the-moonlight lining. The music is delightful, and the film made stars of Kajol and SRK for a reason. They have also co-starred in scores of films together for a reason- they have excellent chemistry and an obvious fondness for one another that shines through in their performances.

-->
Clearly this film has meaning for people; I am plainly not the target demographic of this film. Both of these facts should be be kept in mind when I share that I am not a big fan of DDLJ. It is worth reviewing because its an important historical document, and because Im now living in the UK so it feels apropos, and also its on Amazon Prime at the moment- but in general I find it regressive, smarmy, and rape jokes are never funny. Ever.

DDLJ is the story of Simran (Kajol), a "good girl" who lives in London and spends the month before her arranged marriage traveling Europe with her friends. During the trip she meets Raj (SRK) a wealthy playboy with a heart of gold. While traveling through Switzerland they both get separated from their friends and spend a few days together dancing, singing, and falling in love in the Alps. Switzerland looks beautiful-you can even take Bollywood tours of the country to visit filming locations. This is probably spectacularly fun, particularly if there is singing.

--> When Simran's father hears that she has fallen in love, he tells her she is moving to India, marrying the boy of his choosing, and never returning to London because she has betrayed him. As moving to India and marrying someone she has never met was always the plan, I dont know why he thinks this is a game-changing punishment. If anything its feels like incentive to have leaned into that continental lifestyle.

Aside: this same premium placed on the virtue of Indian women- by Indian men- appears in SRK's more recent Jab Harry Met Sejal (2017), which made me super angry. Sejal just wanted to get laid, but Harry decided to slut shame her instead. Wut.

After arriving in India (Punjab, specifically) Simran, heartbroken, is even more disappointed to meet Kuljeet (Parmeet Sethi). It turns out he is the wrong sort of Indian! Unlike Pardes (1997), where the Americanized boy is the problem, DDLJ posits that Non-Resident Indians (NRIs) not only hold on to their culture, they may be doing it better than the Indians in India. Unsurprisingly, this played well with a lot of NRIs, sparking the emergence of the NRI drama- packaged and sold to and for the NRI viewer- as a money-making genre.

Historical relativism towards '90s pop culture has been in entertainment news a lot recently, most notably in the context of Friends, but my feeling is the same regardless of time period and context: if it is considered cruel now, it was cruel then. Moreover, there were films that recognized that rape isnt amusing- SRK's first film Dewaana () showed that Bollywood could produce amazing movies that actually like women, so saying that DDLJ was a product of its time is just nonsense. Rape jokes are never funny. Ever. 

In the case of DDLJ, and in complete contrast to Queen (2013) and Bandini (1963), the answer is to embrace individualism and traditionalism simultaneously, with everyone happy in the end. Because it is a movie, this is seamlessly possible. Tradition is shown to be loving and flexible, individualism is shown to be subservient to tradition, and men want what is best for the women whose lives and futures they control. Hooray happy ending?
There is value in films which remind us of where we came from, even if we dont want to go back. Stories help us understand our place in family and culture and world, and what it means to go home. But if I want to watch a film about the Indian diaspora, I usually just watch Bend it Like Beckham (2002), Meet the Patels (2014), The Big Sick (2017), or Mississippi Masala (1991). Or the absolutely brilliant Never Have I Ever on Netflix!
